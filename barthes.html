The Myth of Suburban Youth

May 20, 2024 Theory Roland Barthes
“Every word, every image carries a story we often take for granted. What do we really see when we look at the ‘banlieue’ and its youth?”

Roland Barthes’ Mythologies (1957) examines the hidden cultural meanings embedded in everyday signs and narratives. For Barthes, a “myth” is not a falsehood, but a system of communication that naturalizes a particular worldview, transforming history, ideology, or social constructs into seemingly self-evident truths. Through his lens, objects, words, and images are never neutral—they carry implicit values and assumptions that shape collective perception.

In this portfolio piece, the exercise is to decode the myth behind the myth of suburban youth in France. By analyzing political rhetoric, media portrayals, cinema, and music, we can uncover how the figure of the “banlieusard” has been transformed into a symbol carrying broader ideological messages—about delinquency, marginalization, social mobility, and resistance—while obscuring the complex reality of urban life.

Political Discourse and the “Kärcher” Controversy

In 2005, Nicolas Sarkozy, then Minister of the Interior, caused controversy: after the shooting death of a child in a neighborhood of La Courneuve, he expressed his desire to “clean the housing project with a Kärcher.” Following these statements, he went to the neighborhood, “pushed through the crowd,” and explained his comment in response to a young man’s remark: “we are not animals,” to which Sarkozy replied that he was referring to delinquents, not the neighborhood as a whole. Seventeen years later, it was the LR presidential candidate Valérie Pécresse who reused the same expression, speaking of “bringing out the Kärcher from the basement” to justify her political will to “clean up the neighborhoods.”

This form of oral intertextuality highlights several issues: political discourse often presents a simplified view of delinquency in France, associating it with “neighborhoods” or “housing projects,” which, over twenty years, remain synonymous with crime and insecurity. There is a semantic problem, particularly in the use of the term “banlieue” (suburb), which by simplification evokes sensitive areas, working-class neighborhoods grouped under the same term.

Representation in Cinema and Culture

Cinema and culture play a major role in spreading these myths, sometimes deliberately to shock and draw attention to social problems, sometimes unintentionally, reinforcing stereotypes and inequalities. In films, suburbs are often portrayed as dark, squalid, and sad. Youth are represented in extremes: either studying or engaged in drug trafficking (Banlieusards, 2019), where success often means leaving the suburb.

The 2019 Korean film Parasite, widely awarded, depicts a family living in the “lower neighborhoods” of Seoul, in precarious conditions, contrasted with the wealthy Parks living in elevated, luxurious houses. This illustrates socio-spatial segregation. The younger Kims infiltrate the Park household to give English lessons, a Machiavellian representation of social mobility and survival in a class-based society.

In French cinema, similar contrasts exist, as in Intouchables, where Philippe’s luxurious Parisian residence contrasts with Driss’s cramped, impoverished HLM. Again, the narrative portrays a young, resourceful person striving to escape their origins, often in casual streetwear, which signals social and cultural identity.

Historical Roots of the “Savage” Myth

Underlying these representations is the “savage” myth. Historically, “savage” described people living outside “civilized” society. Rousseau presented it positively, as uncorrupted by civilization, but today the term is pejorative, connoting roughness and hostility. Terms like sauvageon continue to label suburban youth, as noted by former Interior Minister Bernard Cazeneuve in 2016.

Cinema and performing arts often reinforce this concept, as in L’Enfant sauvage (1970) or Clément Cogitore’s 2017 adaptation of Les Indes Galantes, juxtaposing classical opera with urban Krump dance, reflecting on social and political marginalization. Rap music also invokes this myth, as seen in works by Oxmo Puccino, Casey, and PNL, likening themselves to Mowgli or rebellious animals to assert identity and resistance.

Youth as Heroes and Agents of Change

Films like Freedom Writers and Banlieusards highlight suburban youth as heroes overcoming adversity. These narratives emphasize that despite systemic oppression, young people can triumph through education, creativity, and determination. However, these narratives often simplify reality into choices between “success” or “failure,” neglecting structural inequalities.

Cultural Influence and Reversal

Today, urban culture exerts widespread influence: hip-hop is France’s most popular music genre, manga consumption is second worldwide, and streetwear fashion reflects suburban youth style. Brands previously excluding these communities now collaborate with them, signaling a cultural shift. Literature and cinema explore themes of identity, gender, and social mobility, emphasizing rhetorical skill and agency to counter stereotypes.
