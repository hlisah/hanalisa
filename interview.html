A Mother’s Tale

Nov 04, 2025 Interview Society

From India to the United States, passing through France, China, South Korea, and India, Mrs. W orchestrated the schooling of her three children across multiple educational systems. Today a teacher at a middle school in Boston, this former Algerian engineer looks with both lucidity and pride at the educational models that shaped her family’s success.

Sunday morning, ten o’clock in Boston. The children have left the house. In the silence of the family office, Mrs. W prepares to answer questions.

Global Citizens

Her son Yanis, sixteen, dreams of law and economics. Jasmine, fifteen, is enrolled in an Advanced Program in computer science. Inès, the eldest, just completed an internship at NASA [Image of NASA logo] . Three destinies unfolding far from Algiers, but guided by the same rigor.

“In Asia, children discover discipline and competition. Students learn to read and write at the same time as mathematics.”

The numbers confirm this rigor. According to the National Science Board (2021), South Korea scored an average of 519 points in mathematics on the PISA tests, compared to 478 for the United States.

#1 South Korea Education Ranking (2025)

A graduate in process engineering, Mrs. W put her career on hold to follow her husband. Fifteen years of expatriation in Asia, five in America. A choice she never regretted.

In Conversation

01. What major differences have you observed between educational systems?
Inès started school at three and a half; she doesn’t know the French system very well. The Asian and American systems are solid, very focused on mathematics. Children start math at five in Asia.

In the United States, there are many Asian students, highly performing and competitive. In Boston, my children are surrounded by South Koreans, Chinese, Japanese, and Indians. Parents invest heavily in education.
02. Was the sacrifice worth it?
In South Korea, educated mothers do not work. It is cultural. Their priority is the academic success of the family, a guarantee of social success.

Yes, the sacrifice is worth it! It is a mentality I have adopted. If your children succeed, your effort makes perfect sense. But the pressure is intense. If you don’t have the level, you stay with those of your level.
03. Advice for expatriate parents?
I can only be 100% in favor. I was lucky to be in Asia: a highly structured, demanding, formative society. But if I had lived in Africa or South America, I would surely have learned something else.

Field experience is the best school there is.
“Their identity is their openness. They are citizens of the world.”
