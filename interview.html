A Mother’s Tale: From Seoul to Boston

November 4, 2025

An intimate account of a mother navigating life, culture, and family across continents.

From India to the United States, passing through France, China, South Korea, and India, Mrs. W orchestrated the schooling of her three children across multiple educational systems. Today a teacher at a middle school in Boston, this former Algerian engineer looks with both lucidity and pride at the educational models that shaped her family’s success.

Paris suburbs, elementary school: students from all backgrounds gathered in the same classroom.

Sunday morning, it is ten o’clock in Boston. The children have just left the house. In the silence of the family office, Mrs. W (a nickname given by her students during her early days as a substitute teacher) prepares to answer questions.

Her son Yanis, sixteen, has already attended a Summer Camp at Stanford: encouraged by his teachers for his abilities, he dreams of law and economics, fascinated by the Supreme Court of San Francisco.

“He loves to debate, he loves to convince,” smiles his mother.

Jasmine, her younger daughter, is enrolled in an Advanced Program in computer science, already oriented toward digital sciences at only fifteen. As for Inès, the eldest, she has just completed an internship at NASA as part of her studies in computer science and astronomy.

Three destinies unfolding far from Algiers, but guided by the same rigor: that of a demanding international education.

School as a Passport

For the W. family, school is more than a place of learning: it is a passport.

India, China, South Korea, France, the United States… With each move, a new system to navigate, new methods, new codes.

“In Asia, children discover discipline and competition. Students learn to read and write at the same time as mathematics,” says Mrs. W.

The numbers confirm this rigor: according to the National Science Board (NSB, 2021), South Korea scored an average of 519 points in mathematics on the 2018 PISA tests, compared to 478 for the United States.

In the United States, the approach is more flexible but no less selective: paid internships, advanced programs, and encouragement of initiative.

“Here, we learn to sell ourselves, to debate, to dream big,” she summarizes.

Global rankings confirm this difference: according to a 2025 ranking of international educational systems, South Korea ranks first, while the United States is 31st—a gap illustrating the distance between academic performance and the culture of innovation.

“Yes, It Was Worth It”

A graduate in process engineering, Mrs. W put her career on hold to follow her husband, also an engineer, and support their children’s education. Fifteen years of expatriation in Asia, five in America, and continuous adaptation without ever doubting the choices made.

“In South Korea, ‘Tiger Moms’ are the norm,” she explains.

This term refers to highly involved mothers, mostly educated, who ensure the academic and social success of their children.

According to a Financial Times survey, 47.6% of children under six already attend private preparatory courses (hagwons) in South Korea.

“I think it was worth it,” she concludes simply.

A Global Family

Today, her three children feel more like citizens of the world than of a single country.

“Inès is the most ‘Asian’ after all that, Yanis is very ‘USA,’ Jasmine mixes the American spirit with curiosity for Europe. But none of them says: ‘I am Algerian’ first.”

She adds:

“It’s the land of their parents, of their ancestors, but my children grew up in countries where often people didn’t even know where Algeria was on a map.”

A dual belonging, both “elsewhere” and “here,” which she now considers a richness.

“Their identity is their openness. They are citizens of the world.”

INTERVIEW – Mrs. W: “If You Don’t Have the Level, You Stay with People of Your Level”

1. What major differences have you observed between the educational systems of the countries where you lived?

Inès started school at three and a half; she doesn’t know the French system very well. The Asian and American systems are solid, very focused on mathematics. Children start math at five in Asia.

In the United States, there are many Asian students, highly performing and competitive. In Boston, my children are surrounded by South Koreans, Chinese, Japanese, and Indians. Parents invest heavily in education: children take many private courses. They are strong students, with an excellent academic background.

2. What personal sacrifices did you make to support your children? Do you think it was worth it?

In South Korea, educated mothers do not work. It is cultural: the husband works, the wife takes care of the children. They devote themselves entirely to their children’s success, more than to the household.

Their priority is the academic success of the family, a guarantee of social success.

Yes, the sacrifice is worth it! It is a mentality I have adopted. If your children succeed, your effort makes perfect sense.

But one must be realistic: the pressure is intense. Today in the United States, Yanis is surrounded by very competitive Indian and Chinese students. There, a good high school means a good university, and therefore a good job. If you don’t have the level, you stay with those of your level.

3. What advice would you give to expatriate parents or those wishing to offer their children an international education?

I can only be 100% in favor. I was lucky to be in Asia: a highly structured, demanding, formative society.

But if I had lived in Africa or South America, I would surely have learned something else.

Field experience is the best school there is.
